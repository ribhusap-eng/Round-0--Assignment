### Final Insights 
#### Insight 1 — Trader performance was strongest during Fear periods
Average daily PnL on Fear days was ~6.7M, significantly higher than Greed days (~0.84M) and Neutral days (~0.16M). This suggests traders may find stronger opportunities during fearful market conditions, possibly due to higher volatility and price dislocations.

#### Insight 2 — Market participation increased sharply during Fear days
Average trade count during Fear was 133,871 trades, compared with 10,813 trades during Greed. Unique active traders also rose from 9.25 on Greed days to 32 on Fear days. This indicates heightened activity and stronger engagement when markets are fearful.

#### Insight 3 — Win rates were also better during Fear regimes
Average win rate during Fear was 41.5%, higher than Greed (30.4%) and Neutral (31.7%). Despite fear being perceived as risky, the data suggests profitable traders may adapt better during stressed markets.

### Trader Segmentation Insights
#### Frequent vs Infrequent Traders
Frequent traders generally contributed more total PnL due to higher participation, but consistency varied across accounts.

#### Consistent Winners
Accounts with positive total PnL and win rate above 50% formed a high-quality segment suitable for aggressive strategies.

#### Large vs Small Size Traders
Large trade-size accounts had higher exposure and likely greater volatility, while smaller traders showed more controlled participation.

### Strategy Recommendations
#### Strategy 1 — Increase tactical participation during Fear periods
When sentiment shifts to Fear, allow higher trading activity and prioritize setups with strong historical edge, as both profitability and win rates were highest in this regime.

#### Strategy 2 — Segment-based risk management
Allocate larger position sizes only to Consistent Winners, while restricting aggressive exposure for Losing or Inconsistent traders. This improves capital efficiency and reduces unnecessary risk.

### Important Observation
The merged dataset contained a limited number of overlapping daily sentiment observations, so results should be interpreted as directional insights rather than definitive causal conclusions.
